A day in the life

A part-time day, not a nine-to-five.

You start at eleven. You take the afternoon. You work again at night. The skills hold the context across every gap so the business doesn't fall over when you step away.

  1. 11:20
    Late start
    /morning-briefing

    You start late. The day still arrives pre-sorted.

    No guilt about the clock. Thirty seconds of briefing tells you what's on fire, what's on today, and where last night ended. You skip the forty minutes of re-reading your own inbox to remember what you were doing.

  2. 12:05
    First block
    /inbox

    47 emails, triaged in one pass.

    Drafts for the quick replies. Flags for the decisions. The FYIs archived. One focused fifteen-minute block instead of forty micro-switches. You're done with email before most people have opened it.

  3. 14:00
    Break
    — afternoon off —

    You step away. The work doesn't unravel.

    School run, a walk, a nap, whatever the day needs. The skills hold the context for you. Nothing is "in your head" waiting to fall out.

  4. 19:30
    Evening block
    /handover · /content status

    Back in with zero re-reading tax.

    A mid-day handover tells you exactly where you left off at two. You pick up Friday's post from where afternoon-you abandoned it. Two hours of real work, not an hour of rebuilding context and an hour of work.

  5. 21:40
    Laptop down
    /log

    One sentence, then you're off the clock.

    Capture where you are in plain language. Tomorrow's briefing will reassemble it for you. You stop being the storage device. The business remembers itself.

Questions

Answers.

What if I'm not technical?
You don't need to be. If you can edit a text file and follow a step-by-step written guide, you can set these up. The pack includes a "New to Claude Code?" primer that covers the bits people stumble on first time.
Do I need Claude Code?
Yes. Claude Code is free to install. You'll need a paid Claude plan (from about £17/month) to run it. The setup walkthrough covers all of it.
Are these just prompts?
No. They're skill files — proper structured instructions that Claude Code reads before it does the work. More reliable than a prompt you paste once.
Will they work in my line of work?
They're built around how a one-person business runs, not a specific sector. The morning briefing works for a copywriter, a coach, or a consultant equally well. You fill in the placeholders for your context.
Will they help if I only have an hour a day?
That's who they're built for. The whole point is to compress decision-making and context-recovery into as few minutes as possible — so the hour you have is the hour you work, not the hour you spend remembering where you left off.
